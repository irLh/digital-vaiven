with dark-skinned goddesses

sizzles on the stove

she fights a battle every friday at seven
dinner is her Vieques
there is an uneasiness in the way
she has tried to season her heart here in the states
there three locks on her door

the spices she does not have in her gabinete:
safety, presence, trust in foreign land

it is hard to walk in truth
when you are lied to
so i am writing my own book of Revelation
etching native tongue into the bark of these trees on

my back

i find myself writing in an ancient language
i do not understand
i am trying to be literate

it is here in this bloodshed
in the trenches of
my hips
and the soldier called Spirit
where i’ve seen battles

ignite

funerals of the soul
a wake for history
grandma’s sopa will
never quell the hunger
that i feel in my fingers
as i reach out for some prophecy
in her rice and beans and tears

a need to remember to remember

called borinquen

and do not forget that Atlas was a jibaro

where her ink stains
the skin between her index finger
and your thumb
blue and black like her arms
words fought for in lead fields
words starting revolutionary supernovas on lined paper
marching up and down these pages
yes, she writes for you
her words are celestial bombs
mapping universes in notebooks
yes, we write for you
hoping you see constellations
connecting dots in planets
her words begin in flesh
where stardust has embedded itself
her words are manna, holy, in the smoke of some burning bush

no, she does not write for you
she has dodged enough paper cuts
and burning comets
to know she loves her words more than you
she knows you will feel for this pen
and you may cry and laugh at the Sun’s writings
but she provides for you
your mortal sustenance
and like dying stars do
she may implode
and she will not care

at Fajardo.

“The space of the in-between is the locus for social, cultural, and natural transformations: it is not simply a convenient space for movements and realignments but in fact is the only place–the place around identities, between identities–where becoming, openness to futurity, outstrips the conservational impetus to retain cohesion and unity” (Grosz 91).

Created from two entities, realities, or spaces, liminal space is where things are supposed to take form. It is in-between spaces, like el vaivén, that “becoming” is what defines it as a place for creation. However, for the experienced colonial identity, “becoming” is identity. To constantly and continuously “become” is predicated on history of colonization. What is being produced at the clash of identities is important in the “becoming,” not what was already there.

In liminal space, the specificities of body become emphasized and hyper-focused. I am made startlingly aware of how my body belongs to spaces and, yet, my identity is made up by how much I have not belonged and not known those spaces. As a U.S. born Puerto Rican, in a very literal sense I do not know what it means to grow up and be raised on the island. I have also experienced a lack of belonging on the mainland, having confronted racism and normalized narratives of colonization by structural oppression. I have never needed to experience the physical “swaying” that el vaivén signifies for a particular generation, but I do know what it feels to form entire being on the basis of sitting in the margins of concepts I know I know. In this in-between space, every breath is louder, every movement bigger. What you experienced is an altered state of being, since the world that you built is only existing through the construction of two preceding spaces The history that I cannot name scars the ever-changing shape of my being.


at Guaynabo.

“dónde estabas?”
my great-grandmother looked at me.
saw me.
with tears in her eyes.
she is ninety-nine years old and she lives in bayamón.
and it feels as if she was waiting for me this whole time.
without either of us knowing.


great-grandma Matilde, 99, in Bayamón.

i like to think that this house made of bone floats
somewhere in the Atlantic.
its marrow is juicy and full of flavor and sits on the shore
of a home that i do not own.
nothing much has changed in this house of bone.
the lungs are still in the living room,
my guts laid out on the kitchen table.
my spine gets caught in between the blinds of the window.
my heart is where the door is, where love and melancholy
and joy, pure joy, and the rain come through like long lost relatives.
i like to think that this house made of bone floats on water between two lands.
even in flames, this house is hard to make into ash. burn this house down,
i dare you.
cremate my love into an urn and put it above your fireplace.
remember me in your homes.
throw me and my house into the fireplace.
burn my bones.


at Fajardo.
